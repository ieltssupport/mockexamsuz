The History of Coffee

Coffee, one of the world's most popular beverages, has a rich and fascinating history that dates back centuries. The story of coffee begins in the Ethiopian highlands, where legend says a goat herder named Kaldi discovered the beans after noticing his goats became energetic after eating them.

From Ethiopia, coffee spread to Egypt and Yemen. It was in Arabia that coffee beans were first roasted and brewed similarly to how it is prepared today. By the 15th century, coffee was being grown in the Yemeni district of Arabia and by the 16th century, it had spread to Persia, Egypt, Syria, and Turkey.

Coffee houses quickly became popular centers of social activity in cities across the Near East. People enjoyed coffee not only in their homes but also in public coffee houses called "qahveh khaneh," which began to appear in cities across the Near East. The popularity of coffee houses was unmatched, and people frequented them for all kinds of social activity.

European travelers to the Near East brought back stories of an unusual dark black beverage. By the 17th century, coffee had made its way to Europe and was becoming popular across the continent. Some people reacted to this new beverage with suspicion or fear, calling it the "bitter invention of Satan."

Coffee was introduced to America during the colonial period, but it didn't become widely popular until after the Boston Tea Party in 1773, when drinking tea became unpatriotic. The demand for coffee increased rapidly, and by the late 19th century, coffee had become a global commodity.

Today, coffee is grown in over 70 countries, primarily in equatorial regions of the Americas, Southeast Asia, the Indian subcontinent, and Africa. Brazil is the largest coffee-producing nation, accounting for about 40% of world production.

Question 1: Where did coffee originate according to legend?

Question 2: Where were coffee beans first roasted and brewed similarly to modern methods?

Question 3: What were the public coffee houses called in the Near East?
